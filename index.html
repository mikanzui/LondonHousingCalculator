London Housing Calculator To: You
What is stamp duty for first-time buyers?

First-time buyers in England pay no stamp duty on the first £425,000 of a property priced up to £625,000. Above £425,000 (up to £625,000), you pay 5%. If the property exceeds £625,000, standard rates apply — starting at 0% on the first £250,000, then 5% up to £925,000, and higher above that. This relief saves most London FTBs between £6,000–£11,000.

How much deposit do I actually need?

Most lenders require a minimum 5% deposit, but 10% unlocks significantly better rates. In London, where the average first-time buyer property is around £450,000, that means £22,500–£45,000. A Lifetime ISA can help — you get a 25% government bonus on up to £4,000/year of savings. Remember to also budget for solicitor fees (£1,500–£3,000), surveys, and moving costs.

What lending multiple will banks offer me?

Most UK lenders offer 4–4.5× your annual salary. Some specialist lenders go to 5× or even 5.5× for high earners or certain professions (doctors, solicitors, accountants). Joint applicants can combine salaries. The calculator uses 4.5× as the standard default but lets you adjust this. Be cautious with higher multiples — they mean larger monthly payments and higher risk if rates rise.

Is a Lifetime ISA worth it?

Almost always yes for first-time buyers. You get a free 25% bonus — that's up to £1,000/year of free money. The catch: the property must cost £450,000 or less, you must be 18–39 to open one, and if you withdraw for non-property purposes you lose the bonus plus a penalty. In London, the £450k cap is the main limitation, but many flats and some terraced houses in outer boroughs still qualify.

What does the affordability percentage mean?

It's your monthly mortgage payment as a percentage of your monthly take-home pay. Under 28% is generally considered safe — you'll have plenty left for bills, savings, and life. 28–35% is a stretch but manageable. Over 35% is risky territory where unexpected costs could cause real financial stress. Most financial advisers recommend keeping it under 30%.

How accurate is this calculator?

The tax calculations use official 2025–26 HMRC rates and thresholds, so your take-home pay estimate should be very close. Mortgage payments use standard amortisation formulas. Borough average prices come from Land Registry data (Q4 2025). What we can't account for: your specific lender's criteria, credit score, existing debts, or variable rate changes. Always get a Decision in Principle from a lender for a definitive answer.
